Deliberate Practice for Web Devs

Quick reference · how solving problems turns into expertise

The core idea: Experts aren't faster thinkers — they have bigger pattern libraries. You grow yours through deliberate practice: work just past your current ability, with immediate feedback, analysing every mistake.

Ordinary practice vs. deliberate practice

OrdinaryDeliberate
Re-do what you can already doPick a task just beyond your ability
Vague goal ("get better")One specific, measurable target
Feedback is late or absentImmediate feedback (tests, peers, errors)
Move on after it "works"Analyse the mistake; fix the gap
Comfortable, autopilotEffortful, full concentration

The deliberate-practice rep (one sitting)

  • Target: name one skill at the edge of your ability (e.g. "solve it without console.log").
  • Attempt: run the four-step loop on a problem that stretches it.
  • Feedback: tests, an error message, or another dev's review — fast and honest.
  • Analyse: where exactly did you get stuck? That gap names your next target.
  • Bank the chunk: write down the pattern you learned so it's reusable.

Norvig's recipe — becoming a great developer

  • Build real things — learning by doing beats reading.
  • Read other people's code; talk with other programmers.
  • Work with other programmers, and after them (maintain their code).
  • Learn several paradigms (OO, functional, declarative…), not just one framework.
  • Know your machine's performance characteristics.
  • Expect it to take years, not days — expertise compounds.

Web-dev edge cases of "stretch"

  • Rebuild a feature you copied, from scratch, without looking.
  • Read the source of a library you use every day.
  • Ship something real and small; get users; fix what breaks.
  • Explain a concept out loud / in writing — teaching exposes gaps.
