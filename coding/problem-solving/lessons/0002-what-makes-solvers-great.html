What makes solvers great — and how a web dev gets there

~12 minutes  ·  The mindset and mechanism behind expertise

You asked the real question: what actually makes someone great at solving problems — and how does a web dev become the best? The honest, research-backed answer is freeing, because it means greatness is built, not born.

Myth 1: great solvers are just "smarter" or faster

The classic evidence is from chess. Researchers showed expert and novice players a board for a few seconds, then asked them to reconstruct it. Experts were dramatically better — but only when the pieces formed a real game position. With random pieces, experts were no better than beginners.

The key finding: experts don't have better memory or faster brains. They've stored thousands of patterns — called chunks — so they perceive a meaningful position as a few familiar units, not 25 separate pieces. (Nokes, Schunn & Chi, 2010)

This is exactly what's happening when a senior dev glances at a bug and says "oh, that's a stale-closure issue." They're not thinking faster — they're recognizing a pattern they've seen a hundred times. Speed is a side effect of a big pattern library.

Myth 2: you build that library just by "doing it a lot"

Not quite. Doing the same comfortable thing repeatedly makes you fluent, not expert. The thing that actually grows your pattern library is deliberate practice, defined by the researcher who coined it:

"…not just doing it again and again, but challenging yourself with a task just beyond your current ability, trying it, analyzing your performance while and after doing it, and correcting any mistakes." (Peter Norvig, paraphrasing Ericsson)

Ordinary practice

Re-do what you can already do. Vague goal. Feedback is late. Comfortable autopilot.

Deliberate practice

Task just past your ability. One specific target. Immediate feedback. Effortful focus, then fix the gap.

Every problem you solve deliberately — using the four-step loop and then analyzing where you got stuck — deposits a new chunk. That's the link between Lesson 1 and greatness: the loop is the rep; deliberateness is what makes the rep count.

The uncomfortable part: if a practice session felt easy and smooth, it probably didn't build much. Productive practice feels effortful. That "stuck" feeling is the sensation of learning happening.

Myth 3: there's a shortcut

Across chess, music, sport, and programming, research keeps finding the same thing: real expertise takes on the order of ten years of sustained, deliberate practice (Ericsson et al., 1993). That's not discouraging — it's permission to stop expecting to "get it" in a weekend, and to start compounding instead.

So: how does a web dev become the best?

Norvig's essay gives a concrete recipe. The distilled, web-dev version:

  • Build real things. Learning by doing beats reading. Ship small projects with real users.
  • Read other people's code. Open the source of a library you use daily. Patterns transfer into your library.
  • Work with and after other programmers. Collaborate; also maintain code you didn't write — that's where deep understanding forms.
  • Learn several paradigms, not just one framework — OO, functional, declarative. Frameworks expire; paradigms compound.
  • Get fast, honest feedback. Tests, code review, a mentor, a community. Feedback is the engine of deliberate practice.

The whole thing in one sentence: become great by repeatedly solving problems just past your ability, getting immediate feedback, and analyzing every mistake — for years. Talent is mostly the name we give to that process after the fact.

📖 Primary source for this lesson Peter Norvig — "Teach Yourself Programming in Ten Years" — short, classic, and written by Google's former Director of Research. Read it once a year. (~10 min)

Your turn: design one deliberate-practice rep

On paper or in a note, fill these four blanks for your next coding session:

  1. Target — one skill just past your current ability (e.g. "solve a problem without using console.log to debug").
  2. Problem — a specific task that forces that skill.
  3. Feedback — how you'll know immediately if you're right (tests? a peer? the error message?).
  4. Review — after, write the one pattern you'll remember next time (your new chunk).
